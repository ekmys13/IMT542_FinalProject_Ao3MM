    <h3 class="landmark heading" id="work">Chapter Text</h3>
    <p>
  <span>The mission is a difficult one, characterised by the sort of brutality much better provided by human beings than by monsters, and Sean doesn’t make it back to the chapter house conscious, half of a fence post embedded in his gut.&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>Marion watches Jean square her shoulders and straighten her spine even as he and the LT lift Sean onto the table in the small operating theatre she’s set up, watches the thing click into her eyes that he knows she built in the war. He watches as she triages–instructs Aunty Bee on how to evaluate and treat the gash on the LT’s forehead, points Marion towards the iodine to treat his own minor scrapes and bruises, and then he watches her roll her sleeves up, disinfect her hands, and get to work on Sean.&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>Marion watches Jean work–he likes to watch her work, and with Bee first occupied and then exhausted, he slots just as easily into the ‘helper’ role. There’s only so much he can do, though, and the surgery is complex–she spends hours just picking splinters out of Sean’s viscera before she can get to work really repairing the damage. When Marion starts to flag, she dismisses him, but he can’t leave her like this and he very certainly can’t leave Sean like this, so he pulls up a chair in the corner and continues to watch, drifting in and out of consciousness for only a couple of minutes apiece.&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>The early morning sun is streaming through the window by the time a tingling in Marion’s scalp wakes him and she’s closing up the sutures across Sean’s abdomen. There are fine lines around her eyes and he can see tension in her shoulders and the curve of her spine, but she’s still efficient, mechanical in her focus, beautiful with blood up to her elbows.&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>Later, he’ll question her, and she won’t remember the last few moments of this surgery. She ties off the last suture and snips off the thread with scissors. She lays a clean dressing over the wound. She wipes the blood roughly off her arms with a towel. She says, decisively and to no one in particular, “That will be enough.” She straightens up.&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>Then, she faints.&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>_</span>
</p><p>
  <span>There are some things that Jean can do with her eyes closed. When she’s performing a regular check-up, when she’s doing minor repair jobs, she can afford to be relaxed, amicable, even talkative. But when she operates, when the adrenaline is high and she has no choice but to be her best, she goes somewhere else. She learned most of her medicine in the war, after all, and the war was a crash course in learning to tolerate stressors that no man or woman should be equipped to tolerate.&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>War Doctor Basar has operated on the filthy floors of trenches, with bullets flying overhead. War Doctor Basar has done the work of what should have been a team of five single-handedly. War Doctor Basar has gone days on end without resting.&nbsp;&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>War Doctor Basar is a machine. She has no weaknesses. She makes running on fumes into an art form.&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>Unfortunately, the adrenaline can’t sustain her forever.</span>
</p><p>
  <span>The War Doctor has to leave her eventually, and then she’s just Jean again.</span>
</p><p>
  <span>_</span>
</p><p>
  <span>Jean is only out for about half a minute, but it takes far less than that for Marion’s shout to bring LT and Aunty Bee in running, the LT visibly armed and Bee–well, it’s an expensive mistake to assume that she’s ever </span>
  <em>
    <span>not</span>
  </em>
  <span> hiding a weapon. But when Jean blinks back to consciousness, with her head cradled in Marion’s lap, everything else becomes secondary to the fluttering of those eyes.&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>“Hey,” Marion whispers, “Hey. You passed out for a second. Everything’s fine, Sean’s fine, but you musta been pretty tired, huh? Just stay down like this for a minute, okay?”</span>
</p><p>
  <span>There’d been just a moment of corpse-greyness on her as she’d gone down, but now as she breathes the colour is coming back into her face. The tension, the confusion and panic, all melt right out of her.&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>When she says, “Okay,” it’s in the soft voice she only uses when they’re alone.&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>_&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>They don’t keep much food in the chapter house, but Bee disappears for a few minutes and comes back with a steaming cup of tea and a big jar full of hardtack.&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>“You need to eat something, you spent too long on your feet,” Bee says. “This is the only food they keep here, don’t give me lip about it.”</span>
</p><p>
  <span>“I would never, Ms Monroe,” Jean says meekly, but when Bee turns away she catches Marion’s eye and scrunches up her nose before she swirls the hardtack around in her tea to soften it.&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>When she goes to raise the food to her mouth, there’s a tremor in her hand. She seems to notice about the same time that Marion does, and for a moment she gets distracted in staring at her hand like it’s a strange insect, and then Bee snaps her fingers and says, “Hey. Eat,” and she does.&nbsp;&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>_</span>
</p><p>
  <span>As soon as she’s eaten a few bites of food and managed to dismiss LT and Auntie Bee, Jean gets back onto her feet with intent.&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>“I’m quite alright now, Marion,” she says, when he gives her a look. “I know you must have been frightened. I’m fine. And there’s still…” She turns her gaze over towards Sean, towards the tools that need cleaning and the room that needs setting to rights.</span>
</p><p>
  <span>“Nothing that can’t wait, Jean. C’mere.”&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>He gestures her over, back to his chair, with both hands, and when she comes he takes her by the waist and pulls her to sit on his knee. She makes this little “oh” sound, so soft it’s barely audible, and sits very, very still, like a small thing being hunted or like she’s not allowed to move until her brain’s finished processing the situation.&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>“It’s okay,” he soothes, not sure what, exactly, he’s referring to, except she’s gone all tense and she’s already blushing </span>
  <em>
    <span>very</span>
  </em>
  <span> intensely and when he says “it’s okay” she usually believes him. “You gotta give these things time to settle. It’s just you and me in here, Jean–well, and Sean, but you knocked him out pretty good–nobody’s looking at you. I just wanted…”</span>
</p><p>
  <span>Halfway through the sentence, he realises there’s no good way to finish it. </span>
  <em>
    <span>I just wanted to keep you still. I just wanted you where I could see you. I just wanted you close.</span>
  </em>
</p><p>
  <span>“It’s okay,” he says again, instead. “Just take a second.”&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>Jean breathes out slow, and she nods. Marion takes the half-drunk cup of tea and presses it back into her hands.&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>_</span>
</p><p>
  <span>By the time she’s finished the tea she’s considerably more relaxed, almost zoned out, and Marion doesn’t find himself particularly inclined to unwrap his arms from around her waist, so he doesn’t. She keeps on relaxing by inches, until eventually she puts her head on his shoulder and in minutes he can hear her breathing slow and he knows she’s out like a light.&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>_</span>
</p><p>
  <span>Marion sits in the chair and listens to two sets of breathing, almost matched, and sneaks a kiss to Jean’s temple.&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>_</span>
</p><p>
  <span>When Bee comes in a couple hours later to check on the situation, she stops in her tracks at the door. She hides her smile with a hand, but Marion has known her most of his life, and he doesn’t need to see her mouth to know she’s on the verge of pinching his cheeks like a child. He looks away, but the only thing to look at is Jean, and–she really is awfully cute, asleep like this. Looking at her, he can almost convince himself that the soft look on Bee’s face is entirely because of her affection for Jean. But she clears her throat, and it passes, and she steps into the room.&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>“Oh, good,” she says, in a voice barely above a whisper. “She needed some rest–I thought I was going to have to tie her down. Jinnah is a good girl, you know, I hate to have to get tough with her, but </span>
  <em>
    <span>some days.</span>
  </em>
  <span>” She rolls her eyes. “Stubborn.”</span>
</p><p>
  <span>“The stubborn ones are your favourites,” Marion whispers back, smiling. “You know, Aunty Bee, at some point you </span>
  <em>
    <span>gotta</span>
  </em>
  <span> consider that maybe it’s you.”</span>
</p><p>
  <span>She swats the back of his head gently and he doesn’t cackle like he would in a louder room. But her main focus is Sean. She washes her hands in the sink, the same methodical way Jean does it, and then checks the dressings around his wound. “They’re the same, you know, the two of them,” she says, checking the clock on the wall and then writing something down on a slip of paper. “You wouldn’t guess it, to look at them. Stubborn. Sweet.” She brushes the hair off of Sean’s face, spends a moment just looking at him. “You know you’re good for them. Both of them. They listen to you.”&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>Bee leans down and listens to his breathing, and checks his pulse against the clock, and does something to his fingertips that Marion doesn’t quite understand, and he sits and holds Jean. She’s pliant in his arms and so deeply, comfortably asleep that she doesn’t stir at the movement or the whispering around her. When he’d pulled her into his lap earlier she hadn’t so much as argued. It’s fine. It’s fine. He’s sure it’s nothing.&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>“This is for Jean, when she wakes up. She’ll know how to read it.” She glances again, slyly, at Jean, and then gets as far as, “You know, when Blue and I–” before Marion throws his head back and cuts her off with the silent impression of a groan.</span>
</p><p>
  <span>“Bee, c’mon,” he whispers, “Not with the Blue stories. It’s not like with you and Blue, you’re just getting sentimental.”</span>
</p><p>
  <span>“Mm,” she says, and then seemingly decides to leave it. “I can help you get her to a bedroom if you’d like, dear. If you’d like to get on with your day or anything like that.”</span>
</p><p>
  <span>Jean’s breath is fanning out over Marion’s throat, slow and even, undisturbed despite the movement and voices happening around her. She’s soft, and warm, and pleasantly heavy, and she smells faintly of the sweet oils she adorns herself with on mornings and of the blood that won’t quite wash off but mostly she smells like </span>
  <em>
    <span>her.</span>
  </em>
  <span> His foot’s been dead for the past hour but he doesn’t dare to move it.&nbsp;</span>
</p><p>
  <span>“I’m okay,” he says. He doesn’t add “this is nice, for me,” but it feels like it suffuses the air anyway. “Don’t want to wake her up. I’ll move her later, if I have to, but it’s like you said, Bee. She could use the rest.” </span>
</p>
