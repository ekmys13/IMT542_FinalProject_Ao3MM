Middlespring 1908

Cards, in a week.

Sean turns the envelope over in his hands. It’s a pale green with a precise and artfully geometric border, addressed in Jean’s familiar, elegant script: Kirke House, No. 5 North Close, South Soffit: Attn. Mr. Sean Finnerty.

Her handwriting holds up on the inside too, where the details are spelled out: dinner with her and Marion at her house in Briar Green— their house, Sean’s brain supplies, helpfully—6 of evening next Saturday, followed by cards and refreshments.

He stares at the letter for a good, solid minute before muttering quietly under his breath, “Shit.”

It would’ve been easier to get this ask in person, not in the form of a wildly formal invitation on fancy stationery. Which is unfair of him, considering that every time Marion or Jean has asked him to come over, he’s made some excuse about having too much going on.

It’s a pretty fucking ridiculous statement, given that their affiliation with Candela has been off the table since they burned their chapterhouse to the ground. And since Auntie Bee smothered Draven Kingsley so he wouldn’t turn into a shapeshifting demon. And since his whole fucking…situation.

Given that he has no idea what the fuck he’s doing anymore.

After months in Stratton under the tutelage of Jiah Park, learning to decipher bakery orders, speed-prep halfway decent croissants, and stop wanting to die, he’d returned to Newfaire with something resembling the will to live, a fair amount of regret, and no fucking clue what to do next.

The savings he’d accumulated from not realizing Park was paying him the whole time, he’d used to rent a room in a boarding house in the Soffit, after Ma had joyfully, tearfully refused to accept any of it. She and Auntie Bee had been crammed into Marion’s old place on the Riverwalk—still are—and Sean has no clue what to do with that information but worry about heights for the first time in his damn life.

He’d tried looking at bakeries, asking around about line prep or cleanup work, but none of it felt right without Park barking orders at him from across the kitchen, or telling him to redo the delivery ledger, or critiquing his kneading technique.

At a loss, he’d fallen back on the thing that usually worked, and pestered LT for something to do. Maybe he needed somebody to file papers, or sweep floors, or clean the fucking john. Maybe he needed a little investigative support—somebody to sniff around where they weren’t wanted or knock on doors and talk to old people whose spouses had gone mysteriously missing. Maybe LT just needed a fucking errand boy.

LT had been reluctant at first, and understandably so, given everything, but Sean had been persistent. Some part of him didn’t even really care if LT could pay him; he would’ve done it for free, for the favor of having something to do. Over an early morning coffee in the Plank and Gull, LT had asked all the questions Sean had anticipated: Is it too soon to jump back into things? Are you all right financially; do you need any help? Are you sure this would be the best thing for you?

Sean had bristled—quietly, inwardly—at being handled with kid gloves, but he’d kept mouth shut about it. Honestly, LT, I don’t know what the fuck I’m doing. I’m sticking around, but I need something. If only to keep my mind off things.

It wasn’t a lie, exactly, but it was a blatant manipulation, and it had the effect he’d intended. LT looked down at the table with remorse written all over his face and relented pretty much immediately. 

Sean had felt a little guilty about it later, but not enough to take it back, because soon after LT had started giving him jobs, courtesy of the Tribune. Investigative support, per diem. Not a full reporter by any stretch of the imagination, but LT had handed him something resembling a press pass and given him instructions on how to use it if anyone gave him trouble for asking questions.

The arrangement had come with a set of stipulations: don’t put yourself in harm’s way. Don’t lose the press pass. Don’t push too hard trying to get into places you aren’t wanted. Don’t do anything stupid.

He’s been at it now for coming up on a month.

The jobs have been pretty mundane so far: people to follow up with, messages to carry. Windows of time to sit and observe entrances to see who’s coming in and out: a hotel in the Varnish, a tram station up near Seasway, one of the docks down in Hallowharbor. LT seems to have a lot of fingers in a lot of pies all at once, working incrementally on learning the truth about each one. It’s oddly rote, despite that he’s hardly ever in the same place twice. He spends his days walking and watching and waiting. He carries a notepad and pen instead of a gun and doesn’t know how to feel about it, doesn’t know how to feel about anything.

Well—except the whole situation with Marion and Jean. Those feelings are pretty clear.

It had hurt when he’d first realized what they were to each other. Not just the ugly tendrils of jealousy that had wormed their way into his stomach, but the certainty of how unjustified they were. Marion and Jean were soulmates (or lack-of-soulmates, whatever). He was at best a complication, at worst, a liability. A wrench in the works of an otherwise flawless machine.

When he was bleeding out on the floor of Duncan Walters’s entryway, he could console himself with the idea that he’d made himself useful. Whatever happened to him, they would be ok. They would have each other to grieve, to heal, and eventually, to forget.

Then the shit had hit the proverbial fan, and he’d snapped exactly as hard as he was always going to, and for a brief period it didn’t fucking matter anymore. When he was bleeding out again with Auntie Bee’s razor in his throat, this time on the floor of the Vault, slipping contentedly in and out of consciousness, he could at least feel relief knowing they would be all right without him.

And then LT had gone and pulled a fast one on him with the Adjuvant, and the rest was history.

Now that he’s apparently sticking around, there’s nowhere to hide from it. It stings knowing they’re living together now, knowing that everything changed while he was away. It stings knowing that making himself scarce only worries them, just like it worries his ma and Auntie Bee if he’s late to Wednesday supper, or LT if he says something a little too cavalier about a job.

He’s avoiding them, and the letter is Jean’s way of forcing the issue.

It’s all the worse for the fucking hypocrisy of it all. Not just the familiar churn of guilt in his stomach whenever he thought about embracing, holding with the same hands he would’ve used to strangle Marion months ago, the same hands he used to try to put a load of buckshot through Jean.

Beyond that, though—it’s one thing to envy Jean for her dalliance with Marion. It’s another to get off on the idea of eating her out at the same time.

Fuck’s sake, Finnerty. Quit feeling sorry for yourself. Sitting in this room and moping over it isn’t gonna do you any favors.

As usual these days, the voice in his head these days sounds like Jiah Park.

He sighs, mutters, “Yeah, fair,” and gets up to shrug on a coat and grab his notepad.

Before leaving the little room, he puts the letter on the bedside table. He can figure out how to answer for his bullshit later.

An hour later he’s sitting in LT’s cramped office at the Civil Tribune, nursing a cup of stale coffee and tuning out the chatter from the bullpen.

Every time they meet, Sean is surprised at how small it is. It feels like it’s all but a broom closet with a desk and an extra chair. Maybe it’s the bookshelves lining every free inch of wall, stuffed to the gills with all sorts of notebooks, diaries, dictionaries, reference books, statistical journals, maps. Maybe it’s just that LT is a big guy, and he fills the space. (That and the ceiling is just a hair lower than normal.) It’s better than the bullpen, Sean guesses. Still, you’d think the frequency of his byline would net the man a little more credit from his higher-ups.

But Nathaniel seems perfectly at home, relaxed and clear-eyed as he pages through his notepad, asking, “So, how are you doing?”

Sean never knows how to answer that. Fine? Surviving? Financially sound for the first time in my life? Not thinking about Jean and Marion, that’s for damn sure. He shrugs. “All right. Don’t know what the fuck’s going on with this weather, though.”

It’s Middlespring, but as Auntie Bee says, the cold and damp seem to subside later every year. 

He adds, grinning, “I hope you’re not planning to have me sit on a bench in the Varnish again. Much rather have my ass warm a seat in the Library or something.”

LT chuckles. “Well, fortunately for them, there’s not much going on with the Library these days except that new production of The Modern Prometheus, but I think this one will be to your liking. Ah, here.”

He tears a page out of his notepad and passes it over. Sean recognizes a Briar Green address in LT’s public school cursive, and a room number.

LT explains, “I need you to get lost in the Briarbank chemistry building.”

“Get lost, huh?” Sean muses. “Sounds easy enough.”

“Specifically, I need you to find that laboratory.”

Interesting. Usually, LT has him carrying messages or waiting and watching, but this seems a hell of a lot more involved.

“What exactly am I looking for?” Sean asks.

LT steeples his fingers. “I’ve been hearing rumors about a new EONS initiative. Some sort of apprenticeship program for up-and-coming Briarbank doctoral candidates.”

Sean frowns. “Briarbank’s full of EONS toadies. I thought that was common knowledge.”

“They’ve always poached talent from the university, but they haven’t used university resources before,” LT explains. “Certainly not laboratory space or equipment. EONS has always been very careful to confine their experimentation to Galvanica or other sites. Keeping proprietary assets under lock and key. So, why change things up? What work is better done at the university?”

“And why not advertise the apprenticeship program,” Sean mutters, studying the address and committing it to memory.

“Exactly. They’re hardly shy about tooting their own horn.”

Jean has said as much plenty of times when she’s muttered in the past about EONS and its business practices. The benevolent institutional benefactor, funding the march towards scientific progress.

“So, what do you think is going on?” Sean asks.

At this, LT’s expression shifts slightly. “I don’t know. What I do know is that two members of the custodial staff who worked in that wing of the building have disappeared in the last month, only to turn up again elsewhere on campus with no memory of what happened to them.”

Sean can feel his spine straightening. Now that’s a hell of a lot more interesting than just passing messages or watching and waiting.

LT must see the change in his posture because he says, quickly, “To be very clear, I only need you to take a look around. Touch nothing, take nothing, and report back on everything you see.”

His eyebrows are knitted in concern, and he’s looking at Sean like he’s already regretting giving him the gig—so much so that Sean can’t help but tease him a little.

“You know,” he muses, “it’d probably be easier to do a thorough snoop at night. Easy enough to come across a crowbar in this town. Or a set of lock picks.”

“No, Sean.”

“I’m just saying. Having taken a course or two at the college of Beatrix Monroe, most places are easier to get into than you’d think, particularly if you look like the help.”

“Please don’t break into Briarbank in the middle of the night.”

“Hey, if I get caught, you know I won’t say a damn thing, LT. I can figure my own way out of it.”

“That’s genuinely not my concern. I’d rather not have to tell both Auntie Bee and your mother that you’re in jail for breaking and entering.”

Sean shrugs. “Auntie Bee would probably be proud.”

“And she’d still have my hide for it.” Nathaniel pinches the bridge of his nose like he’s warding off a headache and sighs deeply. “Just—promise me you won’t do anything stupid. All right?”

Sean lifts his hands in concession. “All right, all right. I’ll get in the normal way. But if I get yelled at by some snooty professor, that’s on you.”

It’s a nice day for once, so instead of taking the metro, he walks to Briar Green, through crowds of suited bureaucrats on the bustling sidewalks of Silverslip, then heading through the winding alleys of Red Lamp, borderline sleepy in the daylight from last night’s revels. He crosses the Briarlamp foot bridge with his hands in his pockets, passing shuffling old-timers doing their midday shopping and getting passed by panting cyclists heading to and from the center of town.

He pauses at the crest of the bridge, looking out along the Stentorian. It’s sunny, but here over the water it’s a little chilly, the wind from the Glass Sea sweeping up the river. He can see boats heading for Hallowharbor: a cargo barge from somewhere inland, with rivermen swarming to secure a tarp in the gusting wind; a pair of rowing shells with uniformed athletes racing against the current, passing a small sailboat heading the opposite direction.

Strange how pretty it is, when he isn’t just thinking about getting from one place to the next.

He wishes he could draw, like Tony could. Or Jean now; he’s seen her sketching before, and he’s pretty sure she helped design Marion’s window in the old chapter house. Or that he had some kind of camera, although the idea of being able to afford one is laughable, even with what he’s got saved up. Maybe LT can lend him one, from the Tribune. He could probably afford the film.

He continues walking, listening to the chatter of other pedestrians, the gusting of the wind, and the dinging bells of passing cyclists.

Before he’d left LT’s office, LT had asked, casually, Oh, and how is Kirke House working out?

He’d almost managed to make it sound offhanded, but Sean has known him for too long. He’d shifted uncomfortably in the doorway. Good. Food’s fine. Certainly ain’t Ma’s cooking, but I’m not going hungry.

LT had recommended the place when he’d just gotten back from Stratton, and Sean had been too tired to look for any other options. It was a bit nicer as boarding houses went: located on one of the lower levels of the Soffit, but they’d seemed unbothered by his lack of a job, and plenty happy with the fact that he was able to front three months’ rent.

Then, a week after moving in, he’d learned from one of the other lodgers that Kirke House was the kind of place that asked for references , and Sean had no memory of giving them one.

That was when he realized he’d been fucking had .

He’d considered moving out, just to make a point to LT about giving a man charity without permission, but the rent was already paid for the next two months, and he didn’t have the energy to spend time finding a cheaper, shittier flophouse.

The accommodations are plain but nice for the Soffit. His room is clean and quiet. There’s a single bed with springs that don’t squeak when he crawls under the blanket, as well as a washbasin, armoire, and small desk. There’s also a full-length mirror, turned to face the back of the armoire. The meals are filling, and he doesn’t hear full conversations or otherwise through the walls at night. It’s…nice.

He doesn’t really know how to feel about that.

While it’s easy enough to locate a set of lockpicks, just a quick jaunt down to the Soffit, it’s easier to sweet-talk an exhausted graduate student with bags under their eyes into holding the door for him as they shuffle out of the building. You see, he’s left his key at home, and he really can’t be late for the night shift again, thanks so much, you’re a pal.

The lab in question is in the back of the building, through a maze of identical hallways with green tile floors and high ceilings, and bright, cold electric lights that are operating at full power even in the middle of the day, with light streaming in through the windows. There’s a section of hallway that’s roped off, and beyond it, the door he’s looking for. Sean does have to use the lock picks there, but he’s efficient about it and inside before anyone can say a word.

It’s odd. He’s never been inside of a college laboratory—hell, he can barely remember the last time he was in a classroom —but somehow EONS manages to nail mad scientist on the head every fucking time. It’s as if whoever does their interior design is reading the science fiction serials in the paper to mine for ideas.

The room is a maze of stainless steel machinery and pipework, culminating in a row of alembic stills. On the far wall, Sean can see a grid of windows, all lined with an opaque sheeting to let in daylight but keep prying eyes out. Below them he can see a pair of rolling chalkboards, and what looks like a long workshop table.

No point in bothering with the stills beyond noting that they’re there; he won’t understand what’s going on with them anyways. He beelines instead for the workbench.

The table is a haphazard collection of beakers and flasks—one or two clipped to unlit bunsen burners—and vials, some containing a pale blue, faintly glowing liquid; others recently emptied and piled messily to the side, for washing, or maybe just disposal. Sean’s never worked in a lab a day in his fucking life, but he has worked in a bakery, and he thinks Jiah Park would have some choice words for whoever’s running this operation.

Not that it matters: scattered in front of the mess of glassware are pages of handwritten: messy scribbles of observations, diagrams, and measurements, as well as glyphs that Sean knows he can’t decipher, but can tell are Oldfairen. Even if he’d never worked for Candela, he’d done more than one jaunt down into the subway tunnels with Tony and Jimmy, and they’d seen plenty of odd shit, even if it wasn’t actively trying to eat them.

Touch nothing, take nothing, and report back on everything you see.

It’s a little tempting to just scoop up the notes, stuff them down the front of his shirt, and walk out the way he came. He’s good enough at keeping his mouth shut. Then maybe Jean could have a look at them.

If you weren’t avoiding Jean like she’s the damn Sack Man.

Sean winces at the thought and puts the idea aside. LT’s right anyway: better to leave the place as he found it and avoid tipping their hand. With a sigh, he takes his notepad out of his pocket and starts scribbling observations and copying glyphs and turns of phrase from the notes.

Fuck-off big stills

Glowing blue liquid in vials

Messy son of a bitch running this place

‘Matter displacement’

‘Duration of transmuted state dependent on dosage’

‘Short-term amnesia upon reversion’

Yeah LT, whatever this is is some bad fucking shit

He’s just starting to copy some of the more frequently recurring glyphs when a voice behind him asks, “What are you doing?”

Fuck.

Sean doesn’t move. “Custodial staff,” he says, casually. “Just cleaning up.”

He turns around to see a short man in a white lab coat. He’s got slicked back hair and a bowtie that’s drooping at the edges, and fear written all over his face.

“You can’t be in here,” the man says, urgently.

Sean shrugs and laughs, “Hey, buddy, nobody told me—”

“No one is supposed to be in here!”

Sean puts up his hands in protest. “All right, point made!”

The scientist’s eyes drop to the notes spread out on the workbench. He steps forward, his eyes darting back and forth, widening in horror. “Were you—were you reading these?” he breathes.

Sean huffs a sigh. He may not be much of an actor, but at least he knows when he’s been made. He reaches reflexively for a holster in his jacket that isn’t there, and then remembers he doesn’t carry a gun anymore.

Fuck it. The man is exactly the kind of weedy bastard who’s gonna be susceptible to a little light intimidation. Lightning quick, Sean whips around and grabs him by the collar, pinning him against one of the stills.

“All right, what is it for?” he asks, jerking his head at the pages and pages of notes.

It’s easy enough to slip back into that kind of persona, to use his height and bulk to crowd into the man’s space, to put just enough pressure on his collar to make him start hyperventilating.

He doesn’t answer, just stammers ineffectually.

Sean rolls his eyes and repeats himself: “What the fuck is it for, the evil wizard shit? Some kind of drug? Poison? You supplying phage dens or something? What?”

There’s a sudden, searing pain in his upper arm, and Sean realizes too late that the fucker is carrying a goddamn phase knife. His grip loosens reflexively, and the man takes the opportunity to shove him and make a run for it.

He follows, ignoring the wound, but the man is faster than he expects, and a beaker full of the glowing blue liquid comes flying directly at his head. He swears and ducks, and the beaker shatters on the floor behind him, where the blue liquid fizzles out and starts eating its way through the wood.

Sean whips back around to see the edge of the man’s lab coat disappearing behind one of the stills as he vanishes into the maze of machinery.

“Goddamnit,” he mutters, and follows.

The man is terrible at covering his tracks. For one thing, his shoes squeak, so it’s easy to tell where he is. For another, he’s leaving papers and broken lab glass behind him like an academic breadcrumb trail.

He’s clearly terrified, so Sean is surprised when he rounds the next corner to feel the jab of a syringe in his neck. Before he can reach out and grab the man by the collar, he scrambles away, overturning a table between them and disappearing behind the still again. Sean hears his shoes squeaking as he makes a run for it, then the distant slam of a door.

Well, shit.

Time to go.

He darts back to the workbench and starts grabbing pages of notes by the handful, stuffing them down his shirt. 

Sorry, LT. Make it up to you later.

He moves to put away his notepad and misses his pocket. It takes a second and third try to get it stowed, and by that time Sean realizes his vision is going blurry and gray at the edges.

He stumbles in the direction of the window and is overwhelmed by a wave of vertigo. Then he’s crashing into the workbench, knocking over empty beakers and vials, his hands slipping on pages of notes. Before he loses consciousness, he has just enough time to think:

Fuck, not again.

There’s a cat in the garden.

It’s a sturdy-looking orange tabby. Marion has been watching it prowl along the back fence with graceful purpose, maybe hunting, maybe just on the move, he'd assumed—but that’s not the strange part.

He’s seen cats in Jean’s garden before. There’s a bird feeder hanging from one of the low branches of the red maple outside of the main bedroom window, and little yellow finches gather on it in the early morning. It wouldn’t be the first time a cat has ogled it like an all-you-can-eat buffet.

The strange part is that the cat isn’t looking at the feeder. It’s now standing on a patch of moss near the fence, staring directly at Marion through the window.

He’s standing at the sink with a glass of water in hand, because he’s dehydrated—because he always wakes up dehydrated when he’s slept badly, or maybe he always sleeps badly when he’s dehydrated. Either way, it’s far too early, the gray dawn light filling the garden and the kitchen.

For once, it wasn’t a nightmare that kept him up. Not the sense memory of the thread of light, pulled taut between his chest and plucked like a harp string in the rift of the Silverslip chapter house. Not the hollow whisper of the thing under Groundswell Station— ah, a vessel, already prepared . Not the horrible variations of the shapeshifter tearing into Jean, Sean, Nathaniel, Auntie Bee. Not the sickening feeling of watching Sean take a bullet through the chest and being just too slow to pull him out of its path.

His eyes had blinked open in the predawn darkness, the sky a dark shade of blue, with the thought of a flier he’d seen on the way through the Varnish earlier that week: Presenting the Ishii Vaudevilles at the Velvet Curtain Theatre, Middlespring 20 – Springsend 4. Walking past it, he hadn’t stopped to read through the list of acts—vaguely he recalled drawings of an escape artist, an acrobat, a trio of dancers, a quick-change act—but he’d seen that the tickets were cheap, and the thought had idly crossed his mind: that looks like fun.

He’d forgotten about it until waking up, when it struck him that was the first time in a long time he’d thought about anything other than monsters and death and sacrifice.

There’s nothing wrong with you, just like there’s nothing wrong with me, he’d said to Jean, and for the first time in his life, he’d believed it.

Time isn't running out for you, not while I'm on watch, Jean had said to him.

There’s a strange weightlessness to the idea of wanting to live.

It’s not as though the intervening months have been without uncertainty or discomfort. Before the summer, he was able to hide his scar under his shirt, and then at least under a scarf or a high collar, after the lighthouse. Now the blue veins curl in at his temples, under the curve of his ears, up past his jawline. He hasn’t tried to hide them the way Nathaniel did his eye, with a glass lens and judiciously applied makeup, and when he’s out on the street, he can feel eyes on him now. No one has been so crass as to comment or react beyond a pair of raised eyebrows or a flicker of shock, but the stares still make his heart accelerate just a little bit, make his shoulders tense up and make breathing more difficult. In autumn and winter, he’d taken to wearing a cap when he left the house, but despite the dreariness of Middlespring, things are starting to warm up again.

But then, at the end of the day, he finds himself with Jean in the sitting room, sometimes curled up on the sofa, sometimes sitting on the floor in front of the fire. Jean wears a glove on her right hand with metal covers sewn into the fingertips like bullet casings. When she brushes his face with her gloved hand, he can feel the heat of the flame conducted through the metal. She talks about getting used to using only her left hand to touch things. About how demoralizing it is that her hair is growing back so slowly, that there are scarred patches of her scalp where it probably won’t grow back at all—and how frivolous and stupid it feels to be so concerned with such simple vanity. About how crushingly frustrating it is on the days when her back has her laid up in bed, and how much of a relief it is on the days when it doesn’t. 

And it’s all right, because he doesn’t need the rest of the world to understand. He has his circle to talk to about it.

Well—most of his circle.

“Absent” is the wrong word for it. Sean isn’t absent. Marion has seen him since he returned to Newfaire plenty, at the Plank and Gull with Nathaniel, or for supper with his mother and Auntie Bee, or out and about with Jean. Lunch in Briar Green, or casual strolls down by the river. “Evasive” would be more accurate.

Every time they meet, Sean seems intensely aware of the elephant in the room, the greater context of his place in the circle now. He’s antsy, fidgety. A little too eager to put his friends’ minds at ease. He talks about his new lodgings, about working for Nathaniel, about his time in Stratton, and between every too-quick sentence, Marion can hear what he’s really saying.

I’m okay now, I’m trying, I promise. I’m good, I’m good, I’m good.

Marion believes him. If there’s anything Sean could never stand to do, it’s let down the people he cares about. But it hurts, the way Sean can’t seem to look him in the eye these days, how he keeps dodging Jean’s invitations to come over for dinner, the edge of discomfort in his grin whenever he jokes about them being a couple of sappy lovebirds.

If you’d let me, I would have you both.

In the gray dawn light, Marion shuts that thought down right away. He drains the rest of his water and puts the glass in the sink, then puts his hands on the counter and lets the cold granite under his palms ground him.

The last thing Sean needs right now is to worry about a silly crush from his best friend. His spoken-for best friend. Even if sometimes Jean makes small, probing observations that make him wonder if maybe she’d understand. Carefully neutral comments that press up against the edge of the issue, the edge of his affections for them both.

They could talk about it. Marion is certain of that. They’ve worked through stickier issues together. But even if Jean understood, even if she felt the same way, Sean doesn’t need anyone’s unwanted affections right now. Not when he’s wrestling with guilt and change and the disorienting reality of learning how to live again.

Sean is alive. More than that, he’s healing. Safe. Home. It’s more than enough. It has to be.

A little self-delusion won’t hurt him. Marion lets out a long breath and decides to let himself believe that. He looks back up to the window, to the cat in the garden.

It’s still standing on the patch of moss near the fence, but it pads forward a couple of steps and makes an odd face, almost like it’s squinting at him. Then it meows, the sound muffled through the window but still loud enough to hear.

Strange.

Marion steps outside in his bare feet, the flagstones cold on his skin, and moves to sit on the iron bench under the window, and the cat walks right up to him.

Up close, he can see it has an odd fur pattern across its chest. There’s a patch of white fur at its throat like a necktie, but where the stripes on its torso would either stretch around to meet or stop at the patch of white, the lines of orange stripes are broken up and speckled with white like a powder burn.

It’s clean for the most part, with smudges of soot on its legs like it might’ve run through the Soffit. It doesn’t look ragged enough to be a stray, but it definitely isn’t acting like a housecat.

The cat approaches him and meows again. It’s much louder outside, and Marion winces, thinking of the open window in Jean’s bedroom, where she’s still presumably fast asleep.

“Hey,” he says to the cat diplomatically, “you’re welcome to be here, but I’d appreciate it if you kept it down.”

The cat ignores him and meows at him again, walking right up to him and batting a paw at his leg.

He reaches down to scratch behind the cat’s ears, and it jerks back with an irritated growl.

“Fair enough.” Marion retracts his arm. 

When he does, the cat steps forward again and continues batting at his leg, but hisses again when he tries to pet it back.

“What, I don’t get to pet you, but you get to pet me?” he laughs. “I see how it is.”

I can think of somebody else you’d maybe like to pet.

Marion winces. It’s the height of irony that the voice in his head making innuendos about Sean sounds like Sean . Marion isn’t sure what he believes in these days, but if the Mother, Father, and Child are up there in the ether, at least one of them is having a good laugh at his expense.

It’s far too early for those sorts of thoughts, anyhow. He gets up to go back inside, to wash and dress and put a kettle on for himself and Jean.

Somehow as he stands, he knows the cat is going to try to follow him.

He’s painfully correct. The cat trails along after him, tucking in next to his legs. When Marion opens the door an experimental crack, it darts forward.

He snaps the door shut, and the cat looks up at him, indignant.

“Sorry, kitty, you can’t come in.”

Another annoyed meow.

“I don’t know what to tell you,” Marion says. “This isn’t my—” he cuts himself off before he can say the word home, because after months of living here, what else is he supposed to call it? He looks down again and sighs. “You’ll have to take it up with the lady of the house.”

He tries a second time, shifting his leg in front of the cat, but it dodges between his legs this time, and again he has to pull the door shut before it can dart inside.

“No, kitty.”

The cat shoots back a long, irked yowl.

“You’re a cocky bastard, you know that?” 

Marion watches the cat sit back and roll its eyes at him.

He glances over towards the maple tree, where a few brave (or stupid) finches are still pecking at birdseed. Maybe he can move the cat elsewhere in the garden. He leans down to pick the cat up, but it dodges out of reach, backing up onto the flagstone path and hissing at him, ears flattening.

He withdraws his hand and tries one last time.

He backs up to the door jamb and reaches behind himself to push down on the handle, easing the door open just a crack.

The cat pads forward, warily, watching him.

Slowly, slowly, Marion moves backwards, leaving no space between his body and the door, wedging himself backwards into the kitchen.

The cat wrinkles its little nose and stares at him like it’s trying to get a read on what he’s doing.

“That’s it, you just stay right there,” Marion says. “Just stay right there and don’t—”

It’s then that Marion realizes he’s made a critical error. Now that the gap is wide enough to accommodate his body, he has to close it again. The cat sees the miscalculation in his eyes, and Marion can see the gears click into place in its little brain. He moves fast, but not fast enough: the cat leaps forward, and scrambles in through the gap between his leg and the door.

“Shit,” Marion swears.

The cat is prowling around the kitchen now, tail up, pleased as punch at its victory over its sleep-deprived opponent. It’s now surveying the kitchen, taking in the high ceiling, the fern green drawers and cabinets, the blue mosaic tile backsplash, the fancy gas stove, the wide island with the butcher block countertop. 

Most of it is a testament to Jean’s very intentional taste. She’d all but inherited the townhouse when her father had fallen ill. The few renovations she’d made with her generous EONS salary had served to transform the townhouse from hospital ward and experimentation chamber to home. She hadn’t made many, she’d told Marion. Not immediately following the war, anyways; it had been considered somewhat gauche, like trying to paper over a wound. Over the last several months, she’s talked with him about wanting to do more, as time and funds permit them: redoing the wallpaper in the powder room, putting sconces in the attic, making blinds for the front porch, so they can enjoy summer evenings without being blinded by the afternoon sun. What do you think? she asks, every time, forever seeking his opinion and input.

It’s your house, he’d said in the beginning, smiling bemusedly. I think you ought to have the final say.

Perhaps, but it’s your home too.

They haven’t gotten around to many of the projects just yet, but Marion remembers the joy of designing the chapterhouse window together, and it fills him with warmth, the notion that he gets to be Jean’s collaborator.

The cat interrupts Marion’s train of thought when it glances back at him and mrrows approvingly, as if to say, Nice place you’ve got here.

Ah, right. The issue at hand.

“Kitty, you can’t be in here,” Marion chides. 

He takes a step forward towards the cat, hand out, and it skitters away around the corner of the island, arching its back and hissing. He tries a second time, then a third, with the same result. He follows the cat fruitlessly around the countertop for a minute or so, intermittently speeding up or changing direction in the hope of catching the cat off-guard, to no avail.

“I know it’s nice, but you weren’t invited in.”

“Frankly, you’re being incredibly rude.”

“Come on now, for fuck’s sake—” 

Soon it becomes clear that the cat has no intention of getting caught. Marion scrubs a hand over his itching eyes and gives up.

“Fine. Fine! You can stay for breakfast.”

He turns away to fill the kettle, muttering, “Could use a cup of coffee anyhow.” Despite the bleariness, he manages not to spill coffee grounds all over the floor when he retrieves the bag from the cupboard. He turns back to the cat, because fuck it; he might as well embrace insanity, and mutters, “You want some?”

He holds out the bag of grounds, inviting a sniff. The cat pads towards him and sniffs tentatively, then makes a face, crinkling its nose in displeasure and showing its teeth.

Marion can’t help but smirk a little. “Not a coffee drinker, OK. What do cats like…milk? You like milk?”

The cat is ignoring him now, padding around him, peering up at the shelf of cookbooks, the fragrant herbs hanging above the sink.

“Milk it is.”

As the water boils, he retrieves Jean’s coffee press from the drying rack and a fresh bottle of milk from the icebox, pouring a measure into a shallow sugar bowl from the cupboard. He scoops a generous volume of fresh coffee grounds into the press. Jean likes coffee and tea both, and she’ll switch between the two depending on what strikes her fancy, but no matter what, she and Marion both prefer a strong brew.

It’s a comforting ritual, so he loses track of the cat for a little bit—until the kettle whistles and it leaps up onto the counter, right next to where he’s pouring boiling water into the press. He jumps, of course, and it’s a miracle he doesn’t burn himself.

“Hey.” Marion sets the kettle down on the cold burners and shoots a glare at the cat. He knows it can’t understand him, but something about the way its tail twitches just feels pointed. “Kitty, you can’t be up here.” He reaches out, but the cat darts out of reach again, back arched and growling.

Stubborn as a fucking Finnerty. Engaging in a battle of wills is getting him nowhere.

Marion raises an eyebrow, then puts the bowl of milk on the floor.

The cat steps up to the edge of the counter, looks down at the bowl, then looks back up at Marion, as if to say, Really? 

“Hey, I don’t walk around on the counter to eat my breakfast. Take it or leave it.”

The cat palavers for a moment, pawing at the edge of the counter, then leaps down with an indignant mrrow. It sniffs hesitantly at the bowl of milk, then starts to lap it up.

Now that it isn’t prowling around the kitchen, Marion is able to get a closer look at the cat, and decides it probably is a stray after all. It’s on the larger side, sturdy and wiry-looking, but a lot of its bulk seems to be skin and fur. Good for getting into and out of fights, and probably not used to getting fresh milk for its breakfast.

Marion watches the cat for a moment, letting the coffee steep, before pushing the plunger down on the press and making himself a cup. Black, two sugars.

Never could resist a little something sweet, huh. Sean, in his memory, grinning at him on one of the roofs of the Soffit. He was probably fifteen or sixteen in that memory, and twelve years later it still makes him blush.

The caffeine is a godsend. He’s still tired, and he’ll probably crash hard this afternoon, but for now it helps banish the fog that clings to his mind like the dunes and foothills up in Seasway.

He eases himself down to the floor and sits with his back to the wall, legs stretched out in front of him. Distracted by its breakfast, the cat doesn’t look up at him. Marion slowly reaches out a hand to scratch gently behind its ears.

When his fingers reach the top of its head, the cat freezes for a moment—then relaxes, turning its attention back to the milk, and letting Marion pet it.

Which is how Jean finds him a few minutes later, still in her sleep scarf and wrapped up in a silk dressing gown. She stops in the doorway, blinking at him. “Oh.”

Marion looks up and says, blearily, “I can explain.”

“You…don’t have to?”

Upon hearing Jean’s voice, the cat turns around, marches over to her, and starts meowing loudly again.

“It was in the garden—it just followed me inside. Wouldn’t take no for an answer.”

“Huh.” Jean crouches to pet the cat as well, but as with Marion, it shies away from her and meows louder.

“It clearly wants…something. I don’t know what, though.”

“Hmm.” Jean glances at the bowl of milk, then goes to the ice box and returns with a sliver of chicken from last night’s dinner. She holds it out and waits. The cat looks her warily up and down, before deciding that the allure of more delicious, free food is worth the risk of tangling with a new person. It takes the morsel from her carefully, watching her for any hint of funny business, then wolfs it down fast and darts back to the milk bowl.

“I think getting it out of here is a losing proposition,” Marion says.

“Hmm.” Jean is quiet for a moment, a thoughtful crease to her brow as she pours herself a cup of coffee. Splash of milk, no sugar. Finally, she says, “We had cats growing up. They came and went as they pleased. We could leave the door cracked for him.” A smile tugs at the corner of her mouth. “He’s sort of endearing.”

“He?”

Jean shrugs. “Orange tabbies tend to be male, though not always. I doubt he’ll mind terribly either way.”

Marion turns to the cat. “What do you think? What should we call you?”

The cat looks up from the milk bowl and squints at Marion, then meows approvingly.

Jean chuckles. “We’ll say he for now.”

Chapter 3: Two Months Prior: Wintersend 1908

Chapter Summary

He isn't used to staying up this late anymore.

A recent memory.

Chapter Notes

See the end of the chapter for notes

He isn't used to staying up this late anymore.

He's sitting in Jean's parlor, holding a half-drunk glass of port and wearing a crinkly paper hat that would feel condescending and childish if everyone else wasn't wearing them. All around him is a flurry of conversation and movement.

There's LT, standing by the player piano in the corner of the room, looking thoroughly distinguished in an evening suit that's probably a hair too formal for the occasion. It's the kind of miscalculation he's been known to make with a turn of phrase or a mannerism, or the way he stands around people with status and power. (The boys in the company could always tell he came from money. No matter how plain his civvies got, the cloth was good.)

Tonight he's got his glass lens in, and he's figured out how to do the makeup around his eye that hides the scarring he's self-conscious about. Sean can't imagine bothering with his appearance that much. Makeup like that has always felt a bit like magic, and more effort than he's worth, and probably he'd be bad at it. Still, he's gotta admit, LT has got his disguise figured out. You wouldn't know he was half-blind in that eye until you saw him do something like miss the table when trying to put down a glass.

LT is talking to Auntie Bee, who's decked out in a burgundy dress that looks not new, per se, but refreshed somehow. Maybe thrifted at the clothing swap in the Soffit, where you could always see old folks trading hand-me-downs for their grandkids, or young folks like Sean, fresh off a factory shift and looking for a new shirt. Or maybe the dress has been seen to by Ma, the way she used to take their old shirts and soak them in the bathtub overnight with vinegar and washing powder, and somehow they'd come out looking brand new.

Either way, Auntie Bee looks healthier, happier. Her cheeks are rouged and flushed from the port, and she's laughing at some joke LT has told. When in fucksake did those two become chums? And at her side, there's Ma. The two of them are rooming together, set up in Marion's old place where the rent is cheap and Ma can get back on her feet. She looks healthier too than she did at the end of the summer, filled out and with more color in her cheeks, her hair a little thicker, a hot toddy in her hand.

Sean would be lying if he said he doesn't still feel a pang in the pit of his stomach whenever she looks at him. He'd never admit it: she's got enough to deal with without giving him more undeserved absolution. But there's an ease to watching her among the circle, hip-to-hip with Auntie Bee, who's hardly left her side the whole night.

The more things change, he thinks, dryly.

They were always inseparable when Sean was growing up, especially after Pop died. But there's something in the way Auntie Bee clutches at Ma's sleeve when she laughs, something about the way Ma pats Auntie Bee's elbow before she goes to refresh their drinks, that makes Sean's head tilt with the beginning of a question that he can't quite articulate, but warms his chest anyways.

And then there's Jean. Gods, Jean. Sean can't believe he didn't know how well the doc could throw a party. She's a whirlwind tonight, feeding the fire, refilling drinks, bringing out trays and trays of bits and bites from her kitchen, handing out little paper hats, making promises about party games once they've all had a bit to eat and drink. Sean guesses he should've known she'd be the type to not keep staff around, even though she could probably afford it, judging by the digs. She's a vision too: done up in an emerald green evening dress with a high collar and pristine white glove up to the elbows, and a matching headscarf, tied up in a rose-shaped knot at the top of her head—with a little paper crown balanced neatly on top of it.

There's guilt when he looks at her too, not just for the obvious reasons, but because whenever she comes by to ask if he needs something to eat, or a refill, or a glass of water, there's a flicker behind her eyes of things unsaid, like if she keeps moving, she won't have to face down the hurt. And hey, he gets it. He owes her more than any apology could give. He's also not ready for that either. The most he can do tonight is what he's doing now: sit still, let Ma and Auntie Bee fuss over him, don't sneak off anywhere she can't keep eyes on him. Maybe he'll never make up for what he's done, but at least for tonight, he can say with his words, with his presence, I'm not a threat anymore, I promise. I promise.

And if there's anyone who deserves to hear that more than Jean, or Auntie Bee, or LT, or Ma, it's Marion.

Marion is posted himself up by the fireplace—partly to give himself something to do with his hands, Sean suspects, and to take at least one spinning plate out of Jean's juggling routine. He's dressed up in his dad's nice vest, and a plain, clean shirt and trousers. Fucker's always cleaned up nice, Sean thinks, fondly. Even in the trenches, mending his uniform whenever they weren't stationed on the front line, getting shelled to shit.

His paper hat is a little crumpled, and he keeps taking it off to try and smooth it out, and every time Jean notices she offers him a new one, and every time Marion smiles and says he likes this one; it's got character. The whole thing makes Sean feel a little less out-of-place, in his least-mended slacks and the gray suit jacket Barkley gave him before he left Stratton, which is probably the nicest piece of clothing he owns now. Not that anybody would judge, but it's nice to know he isn't the only Soffit kid who's somehow conned his way into a fancy doctor's Briar Green townhouse. It's almost enough of a thought to pretend that it's before, back when they were fifteen, or twelve, or nine, thick as thieves and sneaking conspiratorial glances at each other under the grown-ups' noses.

Marion keeps glancing his way now, out of the corner of his eye, like he's trying to decide whether to come over and strike up a conversation. He's not trying to hide it—whenever Sean catches his eye, Marion gives a little half-smile like a greeting, and Sean can only return it, because if they actually sat down to talk, right here, right now, what else could he do but throw himself at Marion's feet and beg for his forgiveness?

More absolution. Marion could give it if he chose, but Sean sure as hell can't ask for it. Not in any universe that's fair.

So Sean sits and watches, and lets himself be warmed by the circle's affections for each other, and by the port that he's hardly even nursing. He sinks into the plush cushions, one elbow propped up on the arm of the sedan, and lets himself stop thinking, just for a little while.

And then, suddenly, someone is tapping his arm, and he's being gently roused to find himself slumped over where he was sitting, a faint bit of drool pooling at the corner of his mouth. Someone's draped a blanket over him, and as he blinks blearily out of the haze of sleep, he registers two things: one, that the grandfather clock by the fireplace is reading five minutes to midnight, and two, that Auntie Bee is holding out a glass of champagne to him.

"Been awhile since the last time you were a party animal, huh, sweet boy?" she asks, wryly.

"Haven't had to stay up this late in awhile," Sean mumbles, but accepts the joke and the drink, and the hand LT offers to drag him upright for the toast.

They all gather around in a circle, Auntie Bee and LT flanking him, Ma on Auntie Bee's arm, Jean and Marion across from him, their dark eyes flickering in the firelight. Jean counts them down, eyes fixed on the grandfather clock, precise down to the millisecond—five, four, three, two, one, Happy New Year! And the cheer that goes up is one of relief, of gladness, of a bad year laid to rest among good company. Ma lays her head on Auntie Bee's shoulder, and they break into song—should auld acquaintance be forgot… (Sean does his best to keep up, but he can't remember most of the lyrics. And besides, his voice is no match for Marion and LT, who could probably hold their own in the Newfaire Men's Choir, as far as Sean's concerned. So he mostly mouths along la-la-la and laughs with Ma and Auntie Bee when they notice.

Auntie Bee says something about checking on Lucas, who's been fast asleep in a guest room down the hall. (Sean shouldn't feel relieved about that, too, but he does; he doesn't know how he'd last the night if he had to face the kid too, along with everyone else.)

LT, ever the early riser, makes a show of stifling a yawn and starting to pick up his glass to bring back to the kitchen until Jean stops him, already bringing around a tray to pick up glasses herself. LT uses the cue to thank her warmly for her hospitality and begin making his farewells. Before he goes to rouse Lucas and depart, he gives Sean a firm, but warm handshake, and says, "We're glad to have you back, Finnerty."

Sean can only nod and smile, and then stumble over a Happy New Year and a get home safe, LT.

And then LT is off into the fresh dusting of Wintersend snow, Lucas bundled up and still fast asleep, slung over his shoulder like a sack of potatoes.

Sean doesn't quite know what to do with himself after that but help Jean gather up the dead soldiers and hover around feeling awkward. The champagne should've made him drowsier, but the bubbles have woken him up a little, at least enough to get back to the Soffit with Ma and Auntie Bee.

Jean insists on doing the rest of the tidying up, and Sean is smart enough to know to listen to a lady in her own home, even though a part of him itches to say, Please, gimmie something to do that isn't standing around taking advantage of your hospitality; please let me repay you, even though I can't, even though it'll never be enough.

He's in the middle of that train of thought, fidgeting with the hem of his suit jacket, when Marion appears at his side.

"Hey," he says, gentle and unimposing.

Sean's tongue feels suddenly thick in his mouth, but he manages a tired grin back. "Hey."

Marion jerks a thumb over his shoulder at the window into Jean's back garden. "You wanna watch the snow for a bit?"

Like we used to do when we were kids, remember? he doesn't say.

Sean hears it anyways. Gods, yes, please, Mar, he doesn't answer, but hopes his nod doesn't come across too desperate.

"Yeah."

There's a wrought iron bench in Jean's back garden that's covered just enough by the eaves of her roof that it's still dry, and they sit side-by-side, nursing the last of their champagne and watching the snow fall in silence, piling up on the branches of the tree outside Jean's window, and the mossy ground cover surrounding it, and the flagstone path leading through it.

Marion is sitting still in the way he does when he's got something on his mind, and Sean still doesn't know how to start a damn conversation, but the last six months have shifted something in him, dislodged some stone sitting in the pit of his stomach in a way that means he has to try. So he does.

"You, uh, wear specs now," he says.

Marion's been wearing them on and off all night—a pair of coke-bottle glasses with tortoiseshell frames that make him look kind of academic. If it weren't so damn awkward, Sean would nudge him in the ribs with an elbow and say something like, You look like one of them archeology students up at the college, and Marion would laugh and nudge him back and tell him to shut up.

Instead, Marion reaches up to fiddle with the frame, adjusting how the glasses sit on the bridge of his nose. "Yeah," he says, "Apparently I always needed 'em."

Not for the first time, Sean feels a distant curl of anger at Marion's family for being too godsdamn focused on fixing something that wasn't even wrong with him.

Marion leans into him and whispers, conspiratorially, "Trees have leaves, you know."

Sean lets the curl of anger dissipate as they ease back into a familiar patter. "Ya know, I've heard that," he laughs. "No wonder you were such a terrible shot. So, who got 'em for you?"

They both answer at the same time: "Jean."

"We'll have to go out for darts," Sean grins.

"Oh, I've been practicing, believe me. Auntie Bee, LT, and I have a standing darts night at this pub in Groundswell."

"Ooh, watch out now." This time Sean does nudge him with an elbow, the lightest bump against Marion's arm. "By the way, Auntie Bee and LT makin' nice with each other—how the hell did that happen?"

Marion lets out a chuckle. "Yeah, they've become a couple of weird peas in a pod. It's…I mean hey, I'm not complaining."

"That's good."

It really is. Of all the things that have changed since he left, LT and Auntie Bee in front of a dartboard together isn't a horse he would've bet on in a million years.

They lapse into silence again, Marion reaching up for another sip of champagne, then smoothing out his moustache.

Specs, darts. Auntie Bee and Ma holed up together in Mar's old flat. Jean's pretty headscarves.

It's a converation they're gonna have to broach anyways. Might as well be now.

"So," Sean starts, "you and Jean, huh? What's it like living on the green?"

This time, Marion is quiet for a long moment before responding. "You didn't read my lettters, did you," he says, eventually.

It's not a question, or an accusation, really, but Sean feels a stab of guilt about it all the same. Marion's letters are still stacked up in the pocket of his carpet bag, tucked away by the bedroll he's sleeping in at Ma and Auntie Bee's place. He's tried to start, he really has—but every time he opens one, it's like the stack is staring him in the face, and his heart starts pounding, and he can't bring himself to even reach for a letter opener.

"No," he confirms, dropping his gaze to his shoes. "I'm sorry."

"It's ok. I sort of assumed you wouldn't… I was kind of writing them for myself as much as I was writing them for you."

Marion fidgets with the stem of his champagne flute, then lifts it to his mouth again to drain it.

"It's not 'cause I didn't care," Sean says, quickly. "I just—I couldn't, at first. And then they just kept piling up, and I didn’t want to throw ‘em away, and the stack just kept getting bigger, and every time I’d look at it, it was just…too much."

"Shit, Sean," Marion says, now turning to face him, chagrined. "You don’t have to explain yourself. I'm not—I'm not trying to guilt you for it."

And just like LT, he means it: Sean can see it in his eyes, but he can't help the swell of shame in his stomach that rises up his throat and into his mouth. "I still have ‘em. I’ll read ‘em if you want me to."

Marion opens his mouth, closes it, opens it again, and closes it again. "If you want," he says, eventually, carefully. "Fair warning, some of the early ones are a little, uh…intense. Anything from Jean, early on. Uh. She was really mad, Sean. Really mad. She’s not anymore, but I kind of…I kind of had to convince her to get it all on paper. pauses I was mad too. I tried to not be an asshole about it, but…"

"I mean…hey, I think I probably earned it."

He's trying for levity again, and this time it's a mistake, because Marion drops his head into his hands. "Don't say shit like that. Please."

Sean nods, even though he can feel the acknowledgement of it tight in his throat and chest. But shit, jokes aside, he does deserve it, every bit of Marion's anger and more.

"I don't blame you," he says. "If you wanna…have it out, we can have it out."

Some part of him wouldn't mind if Marion yelled at him.

Marion takes a deep breath and sits back up, and Sean watches him take all the pent up hurt and fear and anger he's just set on the bench between them, and set it gently aside. Not pushed down, not hidden away, just—for another time.

"Listen," he says. "I've been mad at you before. I'll probably be mad at you again. Tonight, I'm just fucking glad you're here."

It's a goddamn bitch of a fact that before everything in his life got fucked sidways, Sean was always a sympathetic crier. So the waver in Marion's voice makes his throat tighten up, and he swallows hard and nods instead of trusting himself to speak.

Marion pulls him into a tight hug and lets him go just as quickly, turning his gaze back to the ground. Sean mirrors him, and they can each pretend not to notice the other sniffling and drawing their sleeves across their eyes.

Marion says, "Let’s talk about something else less heavy for a sec, huh?"

"Yeah, shit, of course. Tell me, uh—" Sean casts about for something to talk about, something to say that isn't I'm sorry, I'm sorry, I'm sorry. "Tell me how you figured out you needed those."

At last, a step in the right direction. Marion lets out a relieved laugh and says, "I didn't. Jean did."

By the time Marion finishes telling him—and they've both gotten cold enough to retreat inside to the parlor—the ache in Sean's chest is still there, but it's warmed by the mug of hot cider Marion hands him in Jean's kitchen, by his hiccuping laughter, by the light of the fire, flickering into the wee hours.
