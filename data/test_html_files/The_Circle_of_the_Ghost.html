Summary

The Circle of the Ghost Lamp are a particular grouping within the organization, and within the Fairelands. Committed to continuing the work of Candela in their... un-life, they also must hide from the very organization that they serve, as they are the monsters that they would be sent to hunt, and some nights, the monster wins.

Chapter 1: #5038: Newfaire by Night

Not all threats to the Fairelands come from the ancient past, or the deep unknown. Some come from within itself, from the people that Candela has sworn to protect. In their private nightmares, people have the capacity to turn themselves into the monsters that they fear, and unleash those monsters into the night. And while they claim to have control, when they confront the truth, that control becomes more ephemeral each moment. The Circle of the Ghost Lamp, Assignment #5038: Newfaire by Night.

 

 

The liquid burns as it goes down, like a good whiskey. It settles poorly as the scent claws up your throat and threatens to bring the rest of it back up with it. You can’t say much for the taste either. You feel a warmth pool in the pit of your stomach. It fills you, spreading through your veins. You start to feel heat pulse with your heart as the beats start to speed up.

Agony hits when you feel your bones start to crack and grow inside your body. The sharp edges of compound fractures slice through your skin and into the air, then knit themselves back together as the rest of your mass stretches to accommodate them.

You cough up the blood that fills your lungs as your rib cage curls inward, then expands as you breath in and your chest broadens. Your spine contorts as new vertebrae grow between the old ones and your torso stretches as they stack, each one locking in to the one below. Your feet burst out of your shoes— any clothes you were wearing are shredded, the leather was the last to go.

When your skull starts to reshape itself, the pain is too much for the human mind to bear, and so that shatters as well as you drop to all fours. Not that what resulted could be called ‘human’ at all. But the sacrifice of consciousness subsides the pain, and you break out into a sprint, dashing out of the building and leaving nothing behind but scraps of cloth and an empty flask. 
The night is yours. 

 

 

Katherine walked up to the stage of the theater. While the place had been wired for electricity before it shut down, the only light in the space was the single remaining gaslamp. The flame in the glass flickered and cast shadows in the wings as Katherine crossed the stage to the large lever that turned on the lights. It dropped with a *thunk* as she pulled, and the whirring of a generator on its last legs started as a series of lights blinked on and illuminated the full space.

The doors in the back swung open as Gable strode down towards the stage, a tall hat on his head and his travel cloak billowing out behind him. Beneath the cloak, he was well dressed, carrying a cane he did not need and wearing white silk gloves.

“Well, I don’t know then,” Katherine said. “But we’re gonna have to deal with it either way, so—”

“Deal with what?”

“Have you been there this whole time?” Gable asked.

Jade thought for a moment, then nodded. “Yeah.”

Eddie took her hand and finally looked her in the eye, smiling.

“Hey.”

If Katherine still needed to breathe, she wouldn’t have been able to in that moment.

“I am,” both Eddie and Katherine said.

Eddie just shrugged.

Robert swept back up the aisle and exited out the back of the house.

Eddie looked around at the theater, hands in his pockets.

“So which one of you owns this place?” he asked.

Eddie smirked. “Yeah, that makes sense.”

Eddie paused. “Because I’m…”

Jade covered the bite scar on her neck.

“What are we talking about?” Naomi walked downstage from the back entrance as the door swung and slammed shut. “Who’s the new guy?”

“You a cop?” she asked.

“No,” he said. “But I am here to help. You’re Charlotte, yes?”

She looked him over and nodded. The door shut, and Gable could hear the chain being undone and the door opened again.

“Come in, quickly.”

She gave him the address, which he wrote down.

I knew something was attacking girls, so I ran out into the street and a trolley was coming up so I jumped onto it. Hit the deck pretty hard—” She indicated a faded bruise on her temple. “—but I couldn’t see if it chased me or nothing.”

She paused, and Gable could see behind her eyes that there was one more thing she thought of to say, but she shook her head and he nodded.

“Sure?”

He nodded. “Thank you. This will be taken care of, I promise.”

Katherine knocked on a door, her face warped in the reflection of the brass plate that read “Dr. Edgar Lycoris - Surgeon, Attending”. The man who answered looked like he had been stretched several inches beyond his regular height. He looked over the rim of his glasses at the two of them.

“Do you have an appointment?” he asked.

“It’s almost 11,” Eddie said.

“Is it?” Dr. Lycoris said, checking his watch. “I’ve been charting most of the evening— are you with the cleaning crew?”

Dr. Lycoris looked between Katherine and Eddie.

“Told by who?” he asked.

“Malcom Trills?” Eddie said.

Dr. Lycoris closed his eyes and took a deep breath.

“Right,” he said. “What do you need access to?”

“The morgue,” Katherine said.

Dr. Lycoris grinned without mirth. “Of course. Follow me.”

The doctor took them down to a cold room in the basement of the hospital, cold enough that puffs of condensation appeared when he breathed. Metal boxes with doors lined the long wall opposite the entrance.

“Which ones do you need to see?” he asked.

“The women who were attacked in the Red Lamp,” Katherine said.

“Ah, yes,” he said, heading over to one of the boxes. He opened the door and pulled a drawer out, with something covered with some kind of tarp.

“I would brace yourselves,” he said, putting a hand on the tarp.

He pulled the it away to reveal the naked body of a dead woman, pale with loss of blood and days of arrested decomposition. Large, splotchy bruises covered her skin, and uneven slashes raked across her torso in several places, all in groups of four. There were several holes in her right shoulder that went all the way through, also grouped in two rows that lay parallel and diagonal across her skin, which was stained with blood around the wounds.

Katherine clocked Eddie staring at the edges of the wounds where the blood had congealed and still remained with a look that she’d seen on every other member of her circle. She gently stepped on his foot and he snapped out of it.

“What can you tell us about these wounds, doctor?” she asked.

Dr. Lycoris adjusted his glasses.

“They weren’t done with conventional weapons,” he said. “The Periphery were unable to match any of their known ballistics to the puncture wounds, and given that there are no burns, I believe it would be safe to assume that they were not made with any known firearm. The lacerations were not made with a metal blade.”

“What makes you say that?” Eddie asked.

Dr. Lycoris pulled out a telescoping rod and lifted the edge of the skin where it had been torn.

“If you look at the edges of where the skin was torn, it’s jagged and uneven,” Dr. Lycoris said. “A knife or razor would cut much more cleanly.”

“What about a serrated blade?” Eddie said.

“Then the wound would be jagged, but even, or at least predictable,” Dr. Lycoris said. He pressed the rod into the wound. “Even a custom blade has some regularity. The lacerations are five inches deep— if the lacerations on the chest were first, she probably died instantly, though the lacerations to the lower torso would have bled out quite quickly. However… Well that’s interesting.”

“What?” Katherine said.

“The lacerations on the chest end along this line.” He traced along the ends of the cuts. “Which is, incidentally, where the ribcage closes and starts to curve around towards the spine.”

“It can’t cut through the bone,” Eddie said.

“Perhaps,” Dr. Lycoris said.

“It’s not strong enough?” Katherine asked.

“I wouldn’t put it to strength,” Dr. Lycoris said. “While it does take some force to crack a chest, most adult humans are more than capable of applying the necessary pressure. In fact, the correct method of performing emergency cardiopulmonary resuscitation often results in a few broken ribs. The puncture wounds would suggest that this is not the case, as they pierce through the shoulder blade, which is a much thicker bone than any of the ribs. But what is most interesting about the wounds over the chest is that they are between the ribs for the entire length of the laceration.”

“How likely is that to be a coincidence?” Katherine asked.

“Not very,” Dr. Lycoris said.

Eddie looked closer at the wounds on the girl’s chest.

“The skin goes away from the center of the chest,” he said. “Is that significant?”

“It could be,” Dr. Lycoris said, looking closer at the wound. “It would suggest that the angle of attack was pulling away from the center. But that wouldn’t—”

“That’s not necessarily an intuitive motion,” Eddie said. “Standing in front, slashing away?”

He demonstrated, turning his right hand so that the thumb was down and slashed away from him.

“And if it did, the wounds wouldn’t travel up and around the chest,” he continued, pointing along the path of the laceration. “This thing attacked from behind, pulling towards itself, racking it’s claws across her. I don’t think the poor girl saw it coming.”

Dr. Lycoris blinked.

“That would suggest it has a level of basic understanding of human anatomy and ambush tactics,” Katherine said.

“It’s intelligent,” Eddie said.

“It’s hunting,” Katherine said. “Doctor, if we could have a moment.”

Dr. Lycoris raised an eyebrow, but nodded and stepped out of the room.

“It hunts like we do,” Katherine said.

“Yeah,” Eddie said. “Is it a thing like us?”

“In the sense that it’s a monster? Sure,” Katherine said. “In the sense that we’re standing in the coldest room I’ve ever been in and I can’t see my breath? I don’t know.”

“Right,” Eddie said, looking back at the body.

“When was the last time you fed?” she asked.

“Is it that obvious?” he said.

“More than a week?”

He shook his head. “Not more than a week.”

He didn’t answer.

“Unless you want to get all of us more dead than we currently are, yeah,” Katherine said. “We’ll find something.”

She went over to the door and let Dr. Lycoris back in.

“Is there anything else you can tell us about these bodies?” she asked.

“They all look very similar to this,” Dr. Lycoris said. “There are no tells from any particular corpse.”

“The bruising is all perimortem,” Dr. Lycoris said. “There isn’t really a way to give the exact time that a bruise was formed. The bruises do not seem to be either defensive wounds nor indicative of… domestic issues. There is also no evidence of sexual violence.”

“Small blessings,” Katherine muttered.

Eddie glanced at his watch. “It’s 11:30 at night.”

Dr. Lycoris blinked. “Ah…”

“We’ll see ourselves out,” Katherine said.

Katherine pulled Eddie out of the morgue. Outside the hospital, Eddie attempted to take a deep breath. He blinked and tried again, and Katherine could see his chest moving with the attempt.

“It doesn’t help, does it?” Katherine said.

“No,” Eddie said. “I can’t get used to it.”

“Give it time,” Katherine said. “We should check in with the others.”

She stepped out into the street to hail a cab.

“Yeah,” Eddie said quietly. “I’ll give it time.”

 

 

Naomi turned her flashlight to the ground and tried to find something about the concrete of this alleyway that was notably different from the four other alleyways she had been in that evening. She supposed that this one was more brown than gray. Maybe that meant it was made of different materials? She had never worked construction in her life, or unlife, for that matter. This alleyway was also cleaner. The trash bins were neatly stored by the back door of the restaurant they were behind and the crates that Jade was now perched on top of were stacked in a pyramid against the wall.

“What do you think might be here that wasn’t at the other places?” Jade asked.

“We’re looking for any sign of what this thing is,” Naomi said. “Or why it’s attacking these girls.”

“Sure,” Jade said. “But we haven’t found anything yet, and this place has much less stuff than the others.”

“We’ll look until we find something,” Naomi said, her voice sharp.

Figures that Katie was the one that went to talk with people, and you’re the one digging through trash.

Naomi paused. No. She wasn’t going to let this upset her. Katie’s much newer at this, they all are.

You don’t know that. You don’t really know how long Jade’s been like this.

Naomi looked back up at Jade, who was scanning the area from her perch in a way that looked more practiced than Naomi was comfortable with. There were always more questions about Jade than Naomi was comfortable with.

You could always ask.

No, Jade doesn’t know the answers, and Gable won’t give them.

Gable was EONS, he’s not telling you anything he doesn’t want to.

No kidding.

And now there’s this new person Robert brought in.

I know, right?

How much can you really trust any of these people?

“Naomi, don’t move,” Jade said.

Naomi froze, but where she stood, she could see long, bloodstained… not quite claws, more like the fingers themselves were sharpened and spider-like, with an extra knuckle on each of them as they wrapped around the corner of the building.

“Jade…”

“Shh.”

Something like a hand, with five more of those claws, set itself on the pavement, and Naomi could see its arm, graying and tight against bone, with no visible flesh between it and the thin stretch of skin. Its veins were black, winding up the arm to return to a chest that dripped from the jaw to the sternum with the dark red of a fresh kill. The ribs were visible underneath the stretched skin, each a good three inches apart from each other and wide enough to almost give it the barrel chest of a racing dog. But they curved into a deep valley down the center of its torso between the two halves of the ribcage— made all the more noticeable with each ragged breath, and with a well-fed gut that bulged with its latest meal. Its head turned towards Naomi. Its entire face was made of a wide grin with too many teeth.

“Oh, fuck that.”

She ran. It pursued.

Naomi darted towards the street, only for the creature to leap over her and block her exit. She doubled back. Crates fell, and Naomi dodged to get past them. The creature stumbled on the boxes as Naomi tore down into the maze of alleyways that hid behind the buildings of the Red Lamp. She didn’t hear anything following her.

Where is Jade, Naomi?

Her unbeating heart sank in her chest as she skidded to a stop and turned around, only to see the creature down the long hallway of the alley, rushing toward her.

“No, no, no, no!”

She went to run again, when Jade jumped down in front of her and shone the flashlight directly at the creature. It screeched and hissed at the light, and then turned away, disappearing into shadow. Jade let out a breath she didn’t need to be holding and handed Naomi the flashlight.

“You dropped this,” she said.

“Oh,” Naomi said.

Jade looked to the sky.

“It’s gonna be light soon, we should get out of here,” she said.

“Right, yeah,” Naomi said. “How did you know to do that?”

Jade shrugged. “It’s a monster, like us, right? We don’t like light.”

Jade headed towards the nearest street and Naomi stood frozen for a moment.

You don’t like light.

Naomi looked down at the flashlight and turned it off. 

 

                                                                                                    

The next night, all five members of the Circle went to the alleyway where Charlotte saw the creature. Gable was studying a map of the city.

“We’re here,” he muttered to himself. “And the thing came from around the corner, so…”

He headed towards the back of the alley and looked around.

Horatio left the office and as soon as the door shut, Gable collapsed in his chair, and the tension in the air dissipated.

“I think that went well,” Eddie said.

“Certainly could have gone worse,” Gable said. “Thank you.”

Eddie shrugged. “Anyone else here actually been down into Oldfaire?”

“Can’t say we have,” Katie said.

“I have,” Jade said.

They all looked at her.

“When?” Gable asked.

Jade shrugged. “During the day, sometimes. There isn’t any sun down there.”

“How have you been getting down there?” Eddie asked.

“There’s a way through the theater,” Jade said. “In one of the rooms backstage.”

Eddie looked to Gable, who held up his hands.

“I didn’t build the place, I just bought it,” Gable said.

“…yeah,” Eddie said. “Anyways, I know what we’ll need if we’re going down into Oldfaire. I can gather a few things and look to see if I can’t find out anything about this body we’re supposed to be bringing back.”

“I think he wants the whole casket,” Naomi said. “Are we going to be able to drag the whole thing out of there?”

“We’ll have to figure that out down there, what we can bring back,” Eddie said.

“I don’t like this,” Katie said. “I don’t like that we’re doing this.”

“Noted,” Eddie said as he left the room.

Katie shook her head. “Okay, then.”

 

 

It had been some time since Eddie had been in the Antiquarian. He looked up at one of the shelves, the book he needed just beyond his reach and he was contemplating what he would need to get it without asking for help, when he felt something metal poke into his arm. Turning, Cosmo Grimm had rolled up next to him and offered a mechanical grabber.

“I’ve found this helps,” he said, offering the grabber to Eddie.

“Thank you, sir,” Eddie said. He took the grabber and pulled the book off the shelf.

“‘Mages of Oldfaire’,” Cosmo said, noting the title. “Circle of the Brass Ring headed down there soon?”

“I’m with a new circle, actually,” Eddie said, handing the grabber back to him. “Some newer recruits, they wanted someone with a bit more experience helping them out.”

“You’re a good man for that,” Cosmo said. “You’ve seen more than most.”

“I think you’ve seen more than any of us put together, but I’ll take the compliment,” Eddie said.

Eddie thumbed through the index, not seeing the name he was looking for.

“Do you know anything about Vulo the Covetous?” Eddie asked, handing the book to Cosmo.

“Not in here, eh? I’m not surprised,” Cosmo said. “Come, sit.”

Cosmo wheeled himself to the back of the shop and up to a table. Eddie sat across from him.

“Vulo the Covetous was a mage of Empress Iomene the third, Iomene the Unblinking,” Cosmo said. “But he did not remain in her employ for very long.”

“He tried to take the throne from her?” Eddie asked.

“Not exactly,” Cosmo said. “From the research I have done, I believe that he only ever got as far as attempting to convince her to marry him.”

“Oh,” Eddie said. “In an attempt to take the throne from her?”

“That seems to be the consensus,” Cosmo said. “I take it you have the location of his remains?”

“Approximately, yeah,” Eddie said. “I’m worried that someone is going to get their hands on them that shouldn’t.”

“As is the nature of such things,” Cosmo said. “Remains such as his would be soaked in Bleed, I don’t think they would be safe at all, for anyone to store. Possibly not even Candela.”

“I’d rather have them in the Fourth Pharos than anywhere else,” Eddie said.

“Certainly,” Cosmo said.

Eddie thought for a moment.

“What was Vulo working on, do you think?” Eddie asked. “What kind of magick did he do?”

“There are several theories,” Cosmo said.

“Anything to do with immortality?”

The creases in Cosmo’s face seemed to set and deepen.

“Well, that is a very interesting theory indeed,” Cosmo said. “How have you come to that?”

Eddie shrugged. “Seemed like something someone named ‘Vulo the Covetous’ would be interested in. Who doesn’t want to live forever?”

Cosmo’s laugh was dry and thin, and he looked away from Eddie.

“Mr. Ramirez, I have seen many things in my life,” Cosmo said. “None of them have made me want to watch everything and everyone I love die and fade beyond memory. You are still young. Once you start to lose the things that you love more than life, you will understand.”

Eddie nodded.

“I can’t imagine what that would be like,” he lied. 

 

 

The Circle met in the theater, Eddie handing out packs to everyone with rope, flashlights, and other things they would need for the journey into Oldfaire. Katie pulled a small bag of jerky out of her pack.

“What is this for?” she asked.

Eddie paused. “Oh. Out of habit, I guess.”

“What are we going to feed on down there?” Gable asked.

“We shouldn’t be down there for that long,” Eddie said. “Sounds like he only expects this to take a few hours.”

Jade took the group to a single dressing room backstage. There was a large mirror on the wall, with a gold frame. None of them showed up in it. Jade felt around the edge and pressed something, and the glass of the mirror slid into the wall, revealing a stone tunnel that led down into the dark.

“Has that always been here?” Gable asked.

Jade shrugged. “I found it one night when we didn’t have an assignment.”

“That’s a security risk,” Gable said. “I’m going to have to fix that…”

Jade led them down the tunnel, which lead to stairs that wound down, further and further into the earth. The stairs led them to a platform at the edge of a seemingly endless cistern, with columns stretching up into the black, neatly in rows in every direction. The water was still as the stone they stood on.

“You’ve been staying down here?” Gable asked, looking to Jade.

She shrugged, gesturing to a pile of items a little ways down the platform, all clearly stolen from the theater.

“It’s quiet here,” she said. “And no one’s ever come looking.”

“You’re coming home with me, once this is over,” Gable said. “It’s quiet there too, and I have a spare bedroom you can use. Never should have let you stay on your own in the first place.”

He stepped further out on the platform and looked both ways.

“Well.” He turned around and looked at Eddie. “Which way?”

Eddie pulled out his Bleed detector, a small box with an antenna. It had a light at the top and a pair of needles that pointed to two sets of numbers that measured Bleed in units that he did not understand— Bq and C/kg. He knew enough to know that higher numbers meant more Bleed. He took the readings, and the light started to blink heading down the platform to the right of the stairs.

“This way.”

They followed the detector, which crackled as the light blinked and the needles twitched on their dials. Eventually, the platform took a sharp right, as the cistern opened up into an even more cavernous expanse. The detector still showed the source of Bleed was straight in front— through the water.

“Hey,” Naomi said. “That’s probably what this is for.”

A few steps from where the path turned was a large rowboat, that could easily fit twice as many as they were. 
Jade sat in the bow of the boat, holding onto the detector to keep them on course. Eddie and Naomi rowed, and Katie and Gable sat in the stern. Every time they passed another column, they stopped to mark it with chalk so they could find their way back.

“So what did you do for EONS?” Katie asked.

“I was an Alchemist,” Gable said.

“Yeah, you said that,” Katie said. “What kind of Alchemy?”

Gable shook his head. “I was trying to come up with a new kind of stage makeup. Put a little magick into it. Just to make certain effects more realistic, so that the cast could look more like their roles. You could make Jade look like the oldest woman you’ve ever met, or turn Eddie into a hideous monster.”

Eddie gave Gable a curious look and Naomi held in a laugh.

“Yeah, that would take some work,” Katie said. “What went wrong?”

“Well, what I started with was something that would make people look younger,” Gable said. “It’s opera, they’re always casting a 37 year old to play like she’s 19, and she can sing the role, but no one believes that’s a teenager. Plus, who doesn’t want to look ten years younger?”

“I don’t,” Jade said.

“Well, present company excluded,” Gable said. “But that’s what caught Horatio’s attention. He wanted me to push it further, not just makeup and not just an illusion. Something that would actually make people younger. Practical immortality.”

“He wanted to see if you could do what he did,” Katie said. “Make yourself immortal.”

“Not quite,” Gable said, looking away. “He wanted me to make a serum to make other people immortal.”

Katie looked from him, to Jade at the front of the boat. Jade’s back was turned and Katie couldn’t see her face.

“How many?” Katie asked. “How many like her?”

“I don’t know,” Gable said. “I wasn’t even supposed to find out about her. When I did, I confronted Horatio about it, and he… decided I was no longer necessary for the project. I would have died, but he didn’t want that either, so he forced a dose of the serum on me. He turned me into this, like he turned all the others. I got Jade, and we left. I had already contacted Robert, to try and get Candela involved, but when we got back to get the rest… the other subjects…”

“They killed them,” Eddie said.

“That’s the thing,” Gable said. “I think they tried. I don’t think they succeeded. Many of them were dead, but a lot more of the dead were EONS. I think some of them… got out.”

Eddie stopped rowing, and Naomi stopped her oar in kind.

“What do you mean, ‘got out’?” he asked.

“This is what Horatio knows,” Naomi said. “That you made those people like this, you made us like this!”

“No!” Gable said. “I never touched any of the subjects! I didn’t even know it was being tested on people!”

“How could you not?” Katie asked. “What, they were testing a human immortality serum on rats?”

“Horatio lied to me!” Gable said. “The key ingredient, the thing that makes us this, it was something he said he had limited supply of, but he let me use it for this. He never told me what it actually did, he just kept coming back with more adjustments I should make, I didn’t know!”

“And we’re supposed to believe that?” Naomi asked.

“That’s why you’re so certain you can find a cure,” Katie said.

“Whoa, what?” Eddie said. “Who said anything about a cure?”

“Of course we want a cure!” Naomi said. “You’re working on one?”

“I am,” Gable said. “The notes I got from Dr. Jewel’s lab are about that as well. I’ll test it on myself if I have to—”

“Absolutely not!” Eddie said. “Best case scenario, you turn into that thing we killed, and I don’t even want to think about what would be the worst case. A cure? That’s never how these things work. This isn’t some disease, this is Bleed and you don’t come back from Bleed. It takes you and chips more and more of you away until you’re a husk of what you once were, and only then does it kill you. And all of us are just unlucky enough to survive being dead. Gable, this isn’t your fault. But I’m not letting you turn yourself into a monster, because I refuse to be the person that has to kill you.”

He started rowing again, looking down at the boards of the boat, rather than anyone else. Naomi rowed with him, looking to her sister.

“If there is a cure, we’re getting it, right?” Naomi asked.

“Yeah,” Katie said. “Sure.”

“I’ll kill you if you turn into a monster, Gable,” Jade said.

Gable smiled. “Thanks, Jade.”

As they continued to follow the source of the Bleed, they came upon a mausoleum made of white marble, rising up from the waters, with nothing around it. It was surrounded by a colonnade, with steps leading up to iron doors from the water, enough that when they got out, Eddie and Naomi could moor the boat on the steps to keep it from drifting away. Katie held up a flashlight to read the inscriptions in Ancient Fairen.

“Vulo the Covetous,” she read. “Traitor to the Empress. May no rest find him worthy.”

“Sounds like a great guy,” Naomi said, pulling a crowbar out of her bag. “Let’s rob him.”

It took all five of them to budge the door to the point where it could open. It swung out and hit the marble wall with a dull thud that echoed through the cistern.

The interior of the mausoleum was stark and austere. The same message on the door was inscribed on the back wall in gold. At the center of the room was a large marble coffin, with Vulo’s name carved into it.

“I don’t think that’s going back on the boat,” Jade said.

Eddie ran a hand along the side, feeling for a seam in the marble.

“The top comes off,” he said.

Eddie, Naomi, and Gable pushed the marble lid of the coffin off of the top. It fell on the other side, shattering on the marble floor. The Bleed detector went wild. Looking in, the marble had been lined with lead and gold, and further inside was a wooden box, still large enough for a body.

With a loud thud, the box moved.

They all jumped back.

“So he’s still alive,” Katie said.

“Yeah,” Eddie said.

“Do we kill him?” Naomi asked. “We’re looking for a body, not a person.”

“I think we found exactly what Horatio wanted us to find,” Gable said. “Damn it!”

He started to pace, and Jade moved closer to the coffin, putting a hand on the wood.

Eddie stepped forward. “Jade, don’t—”

She held up her other hand. “I can do this.”

She actively took a deep breath, forcing air in and out of her lungs. Her eyes closed. A green haze started to rise from the wood and wound up her arm, surrounding her, and then pouring into her eyes. She yelled and lost her balance, falling backwards. Eddie caught her before she hit her head.

“We should leave,” he said.

“No,” Jade said, the green haze whisping out of her mouth. “No, he needs help.”

Her eyes glowed and she reached out towards the coffin.

“He needs help.”

She scrambled to her feet, taking the crowbar from Naomi, and she climbed into the marble box. The box had stilled long enough for her to pry the top off and something shot out at her and everyone yelled.

“Stop!”

Everyone paused at Jade’s direction. Standing in front of her was a young man, probably not much older than she was. He wore a simple Oldfairen outfit, weathered by time. His skin was pale, with a scar along the side of his face that was mostly hidden by long, blond hair. He stared directly at Jade, who gently reached out to touch the scar. He braced himself on the lip of the marble, hands on either side of Jade, leaned forward, and pressed his forehead against hers.

“We’re okay,” Jade said quietly. “You’re okay.”

He spoke in a language none of them had heard before, but could only have been Oldfairen. His voice was quiet and shaky, hoarse from disuse.

“Yeah,” Jade said.

Eddie looked over the young man. His hands were scarred and callused, his fingernails torn and in the process of regrowing.

“He’s been trying to escape all these years,” Eddie said.

What Katie noticed was that the young man wasn’t breathing.

“Gable, I don’t think you’re the first person Horatio has done this to,” Katie said.

The young man confirmed this by leaning over and biting Jade’s neck.

“Shit!” Eddie rushed forward and attempted to pull the young man off of Jade.

“Eddie, don’t!” Jade said.

She held tight to the young man.

“I’m okay,” she said. “It’s okay, he won’t do anything.”

The young man drank, and Jade’s eyes pleaded with Eddie to let him continue. He didn’t take his hands off the young man, but stopped trying to pull him away. When the young man finished, Jade stumbled, light-headed, and Eddie moved to keep her standing.

“Who… are… you?” the young man asked.

“I’m Jade,” she said. “You’re safe now.”

The young man nodded and collapsed, unconscious.

“Okay, what the fuck?” Katie said. “Is that Vulo?”

“No,” Jade said. “Or if it is, he doesn’t remember.”

“Can’t be,” Gable said. “The person you were describing, Eddie, that had to be someone older.”

“I didn’t find out much,” Eddie admitted. “It could be, though.”

“Horatio just wants the box, though, right?” Naomi said.

“I think he wanted the kid,” Eddie said.

He picked up the young man, throwing him over his shoulder.

“We’ll bring the box, too,” he said. “Let’s head back.”

 

 

Horatio stepped into Gable’s office. The wooden coffin was on the floor, open and empty. He looked around and noticed someone was missing.

“Where is Jade?” he asked.

“Not here,” Eddie said.

“We brought back your thing,” Katie said.

“I remember asking for remains,” Horatio said. “Not the box that held them. Where is the body?”

“There weren’t any remains,” Gable said, stepping around his desk. “And I think you knew that when you sent us down there.”

“Oh?” Horatio said.

“Who was in the box, Horatio?” Gable asked. “Or should I call you Vulo?”

Horatio grinned. “Think of me what you will, Gable, but I am not Vulo the Covetous.”

“No, but you know what he was,” Gable said. “He was like what you made me to be, wasn’t he? That’s how he betrayed the Empress, he was trying to make her like him. Like us.”

“Interesting theory,” Horatio said.

“Vulo managed to trick someone else into receiving his punishment, didn’t he?” Gable asked.

“Well,” Horatio said. “If he had done that, why would he share that information?”

“Because he was bragging to you about how he escaped,” Naomi said.

Horatio paused. “What are you accusing me of?”

“That last ingredient you had me put in the serum, all those years ago,” Gable said. “That was Vulo’s blood, wasn’t it? And you’ve finally run out of it, haven’t you? But! If Vulo had turned someone— an assistant, someone that wouldn’t be missed, and made sure that assistant was in the coffin and not him, wouldn’t that be close enough to what you need? And when EONS shut down your factory, you couldn’t track down the survivors, and you ran out of Bleed-tainted blood, you knew exactly where to get more of it. And it solved two problems at once— get rid of the last people who knew what you did, and, if you were lucky, six more bodies to exsanguinate.”

“You seem to have all this wrapped up quite nicely,” Horatio said. “I can just leave the money.”

“Oh, we’ll be taking your money,” Katie said. “Just, when Gable’s done with you.”

“Excuse m—” Horatio was cut off by Gable grabbing his throat with one hand, crushing his windpipe.

“Thing is, you only went for immortality,” Eddie said. “We got a few more tricks than that.”

Gable slammed Horatio into the wall.

“You. Can’t. Kill. Me.” Horatio choked out.

“I’d love to test that theory,” Gable said before biting into his neck. 

 

                                                                                                    

The young man shot up from sleep, gasping for air he didn’t need, and Jade rushed over to him.

“It’s okay!” she said. “You’re safe now.”

The young man looked around the small room. He was lying on a long piece of padded furniture, a blanket covering him up. He didn’t recognize much of anything in the room.

“Where am I?” he asked, the language of these people still new on his tongue.

“The Williams Theater,” she said. “In Newfaire.”

“New Faire?” he asked.

“Right,” she said, nodding. “You were in that box for a while, huh?”

“Yes,” he said. “How long?”

“Oh, I don’t know that,” Jade said. “Katie might know. Centuries at least.”

“Oh.”

He looked away, thinking for a moment.

“How am I alive?” he asked.

“We think Vulo the Covetous turned you into a monster,” Jade said.

He blinked.

“Not a scary one, just a kinda human-shaped one? We’re all monsters too, it’s okay,” she said.

“That’s… not…” He took a moment to find the word. “Comforting?”

“Yeah,” she said. “But, hey, we can get you some new clothes, you can stay here, and we’ll figure it out.”

“How am I speaking like this?” he asked.

“Um… I have some things I can do, because of the Bleed I have?” Jade said. “Not like the others— like, Eddie can make people pay attention to him, like really pay attention to him, and Gable’s really good with potions and stuff, and—”

“So, magick?” he said.

“Yeah, magick!” Jade said, smiling.

“Okay,” he said, nodding. “Vulo, I know that name…”

“Great!” Jade said. “Is it your name?”

“No,” he said. “I don’t… I don’t remember mine.”

“That’s okay,” she said. “We’ll give you one, once the others are done with whatever they’re doing.”

At that moment, the door to the room opened and Gable stepped in. His suit was wrecked— wrinkled and torn, with his white shirt nearly red with blood. The young man yelled and tried to move away and Jade went to calm him down.

“It’s okay, you’re safe,” she said.

“Hey,” Gable said. “I didn’t think he’d be awake, um, but— we wanted to use your box? The one we found you in, is that okay?”

“I never want to see that box again,” the young man said.

“Great, we’re just going to dump it in the water downstairs,” Gable said. He then looked to Jade. “Are you okay?”

“Yeah,” Jade said. “Did you get the money?”

“Yes,” Gable said. “And Horatio is never going to bother us again, I can promise that.”

“Great,” Jade said, grinning.

The young man looked between the two of them.

What are they going to use the box for, do you think?

Gable left and Jade was alone with him again.

“What now?” he asked.

“I don’t know,” Jade said. “But we’ll figure it out.”

“And we’re safe here?” he asked.

“Yeah,” Jade said.

For now.
